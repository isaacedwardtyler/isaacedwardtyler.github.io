Methodology


In our Model for the 5GDH, there 5 available potential sources in Wallacetown that was suggested to be used within the network for both energy supply and storage.

Waste Water Heat Recovery Systems

This system offers both individual and communal options, allowing for the recovery of heat from various household and industrial activities. In Scotland, it is estimated that approximately 150 litters of wastewater per person per day is generated [​1]​. The wastewater inputs into the system at an average temperature of 15°C in winter and 25°C in summer [​2]​, with an estimated flow rate of 4.6 litters per second from Wallacetown. The potential for wastewater heat recovery is substantial. For example, in London alone, it is estimated that around 4TWh of heat could be recovered annually from the city's wastewater treatment plants. By capturing and utilizing this waste heat, we can significantly reduce energy consumption and associated emissions [​2]​.

Water Source Heat Pumps

Generally, these highly efficient systems can achieve efficiencies ranging from 300% to 600%​[3]​. They operate by passing water through heat pumps to extract thermal energy from a closed-loop system that abstracts water from the river Ayr. WSHPs have the capability to deliver water temperatures up to 85°C, making them suitable for district heating applications [​4]​. The discharged water is then returned to the river at a lower temperature, generating less CO2 emissions compared to traditional systems.

The river Ayr has a catchment area of 574 square kilometres, with water temperatures ranging from 7°C in February to 16°C in August [​5]​. Solar Thermal

Ayr receives approximately 1000 kWh/m^2 of global horizontal irradiance, making it a viable option for solar thermal energy generation [​6]​. Literature suggests that an area of over 1000 square meters per 50 houses is required for feasibility. Any excess heat generated will be stored in thermal storage (boreholes) for later use. Solar thermal installations would be located on a selected site, like the borehole area Prestwick solar data that shows that direct solar increases up to over 450W/m2 between May and August ​​[7]​.

Abandoned Mine Works

Wallacetown has flooded abandoned mines with an upper estimated volume of 100,000 cubic meters. These abandoned mines can serve as both an energy supply source and a storage facility. The mine water maintains stable temperatures ranging from 10°C to 40°C at a depth of 1 kilometre. This water can be utilized to provide heating and cooling for the district heating system [​8,9,10]​.

Map shows where mine works are in Wallacetown within the scope area and figure 9 that show how the energy is extracted from the mines using heat pumps.

Thermal Sources Selection

For the model, we have carefully evaluated various thermal sources and selected the most suitable options based on their compatibility, energy potential, and feasibility:
Solar thermal, river water source heat pump and seasonal thermal storage met the criteria.
Mine Water Source Heat Pump pose challenges due to limited data availability and the risk of failure.
Wastewater Heat Recovery System could contribute to the system, but the amount of energy it can provide is relatively low compared to other sources. Additionally, all centralised treatment sites are located outside the scope area, making it less feasible for integration.
